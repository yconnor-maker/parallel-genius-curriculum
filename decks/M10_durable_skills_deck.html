The Question

Most "AI skills" will be obsolete in 6 months. Which ones survive?

The tools will change. ChatGPT will be replaced. Claude will evolve. New models will emerge. The specific interfaces, prompts, and workarounds you learn today will break tomorrow.

So what's worth investing in?

From "I Don't Use Prompts"
"The whole framing of 'prompt engineering' suggests that the human's job is to figure out how to talk to the machine. I think it's the opposite. The right framing is: how do I think clearly enough that the machine becomes useful?"— Dr. Yamicia Connor, "I Don't Use Prompts"
04
Durable Skill 1

Thinking Out Loud (Dictation)

This survives every tool change because it's not about the tool — it's about clarifying your own thinking. Whether you're talking to GPT-4, GPT-7, or a system that hasn't been invented yet, the ability to articulate unstructured thoughts and have them reflected back is the foundation.

Why It's Durable

Tools change. Interfaces change. The human need to process complexity through speech doesn't. Surgeons, lawyers, and executives have been dictating for 100 years. AI just makes the reflection instantaneous.

05
Durable Skill 2

System Architecture

The five-stage pipeline, permission models, boot sequences — these are system design skills. They transfer to any tool, any platform, any future model.

Why It's Durable

The need for structure doesn't go away when the tool improves. If anything, better AI tools produce MORE output, making architecture MORE important, not less. Without a pipeline, better AI just means faster chaos.

06
Durable Skill 3

Precision Framing

Asking the right question. This is the skill that makes every other skill effective. It transfers perfectly because it's about human cognition, not machine behavior.

"Every research question has embedded assumptions. Most of them are invisible because they were never made explicit. Making them explicit is the work."— Dr. Yamicia Connor, "How to Ask the Right Question"
07
The Meta-Skill

The Ability to Learn New Tools Quickly

The most durable skill of all: the meta-skill of rapidly adapting to new tools. This curriculum teaches principles, not procedures.

→

Principle: every task must be self-contained

The TOOL for writing task specs will change. The principle won't.

→

Principle: the human needs visual formats

The TOOL for creating slides will change. The human brain won't.

→

Principle: knowledge dies when sessions end

The TOOL for extraction will change. The need won't.
