01
The Problem

Everyone is asking the wrong question.

The entire AI productivity industry is organized around one question:

"How do I write a better prompt?"

That's the wrong question. The skill that actually determines AI quality is something surgeons learn in residency and almost nobody else is taught.

02
Ask the audience: "How many of you have searched for 'best AI prompts' or 'prompt engineering tips'? Show of hands." Almost everyone will raise their hand. "That's the industry talking. Today we're going to unlearn that."
"I don't use prompts. I talk to the AI the way I talk to a colleague. I explain what I'm trying to do. I think out loud. I contradict myself. I explore tangents. I come back to the main thread. I argue. I ask it to push back. I tell it when it's wrong. I tell it when I'm wrong." — Dr. Yamicia Connor, "I Don't Use Prompts"

The Insight

This is the skill that transfers directly to AI.

"Not prompt engineering. Dictation. The ability to talk to a machine and have your thinking reflected back to you in structured form." — Dr. Yamicia Connor

Most people have never been trained to think out loud systematically. Most people think in their heads and write with their fingers.

05
Key Distinction

Prompting vs. Dictation

❌ Prompting

  • A transaction: input → output
  • Polished, pre-constructed
  • One-shot framing
  • Optimized for the machine
  • Answer-seeking
vs.

✅ Dictation

  • A conversation: think ↔ reflect ↔ refine
  • Messy, half-formed, honest
  • Iterative deepening
  • Optimized for human thinking
  • Understanding-seeking

The quality difference is not marginal — it is categorical.

06
The Barrier

Vulnerability is the feature, not the bug.

"The idea of speaking your unfinished thoughts into a system and having them organized, challenged, and refined feels alien. It feels vulnerable. It feels like you're exposing incomplete thinking."

But that's exactly the point.

AI is most useful when you give it your incomplete thinking and let it help you complete it. Not your polished prompt. Your messy, contradictory, half-formed ideas.

07

Lessons from One Year

What Actually Matters

1

You have to bring the thinking

"AI quality is proportional to the quality of thinking you put in. This is why the 'anyone can use AI' narrative is misleading."

2

You have to build verification in

"I use AI to check AI — one tool validates another's output."

3

You have to teach it your voice

"The quality of AI output is proportional to how much of your own language you pour into it."

4

You have to accept you'll waste time

"An hour lost, not a week. A bad draft, not a bad hire."

5

You have to let go of what ownership means

"I never once thought my research was solely mine because I had a research assistant. AI is the same."

Proof of Concept

"This article was dictated while managing three children, preparing dinner, and navigating an interruption involving hot milk."

That's not a humble brag. That's an engineering specification.

If the method works while managing chaos, it works. If it requires quiet focus and an hour of prep time, it's a tool for people who already have time to think. The dictation method works because it meets professionals where they actually are.

Workshop

Try It Right Now

⏱ 5 Minutes

Exercise: Your First Dictation

1

Pick a work problem

Something you're currently stuck on. Not a simple question — a messy, unresolved problem.

2

Record yourself talking about it for 5 minutes

Don't write it — speak it. Use your phone. Talk through what you're trying to do, why it's hard, what you've tried.

3

Feed the transcript into an AI

Ask: "What am I actually trying to solve here?"

4

Compare

Compare the AI's reflection to what you would have written if you'd started with a blank page. What did you discover?

14
Objections

What You're Probably Thinking

  • "I don't have anything worth dictating." — You do. You have half-formed ideas that die because you can't iterate fast enough. That's exactly what dictation captures.
  • "I'll sound stupid." — No one hears it but the machine. And the machine doesn't judge. It reflects.
  • "I already write good prompts." — Great. Now try having a 20-minute conversation instead. See what surfaces that your prompt missed.
  • "This won't work for my field." — This method was invented in operating rooms and now runs a media company. The transfer is the point.
15
The Bigger Picture

The Skill They Should Be Teaching in Grade School

If dictation — the ability to think out loud in structured form — is the most important AI skill, then we have an education problem:

  • Schools teach writing (fingers on keyboard)
  • Schools don't teach structured speaking (thoughts into system)
  • The cognitive skill underneath both is the same: organizing thinking in real time
  • But the interface changed, and the training didn't

This is why "anyone can use AI" is misleading. Anyone can. Not everyone will use it well. It requires training most people have never received.
