Module 07

The Production Loop

Idea to Deliverable — The 70% Gap

Track: Building Your AI System Duration: 90 min Format: Lecture + Workshop

The Argument

Core Thesis

AI gets you 70% of the way there. Fast. Impressively fast. But the last 30% — the quality control, the human judgment, the clinical accuracy, the deployment — is where all the value lives. And that's where most people stall.

The production loop is the system that bridges the gap between "AI produced something" and "something is published, deployed, or delivered." Without it, you accumulate an ever-growing pile of 70%-done work that nobody ever sees.

"I completed seventy articles in two afternoons using Claude's Cowork feature. My team couldn't absorb it. They're still working through the backlog."

The Phases

Three Phases of Knowledge Work

Phase 1: Research & Design

AI accelerates this 10x. Literature review, data analysis, option generation, framework building — AI is extraordinary here. This is where most people start and where most people get stuck, because producing research feels like producing work.

The trap: Spending 3 hours with AI researching the perfect approach and never building anything.

Phase 2: Refinement

This is where the Dictation Method matters most. Raw AI output becomes a draft. The draft gets human feedback. The feedback generates a revision. This is the back-and-forth that turns 70% into 95%. It's the most time-consuming phase, and the one AI compresses most effectively.

The trap: Accepting the first draft. "Good enough" is the enemy of quality.

Phase 3: Execution

Where AI falls apart and the real work lives. Deployment. Publication. QC. Formatting. Distribution. These are logistics — and they require tools, permissions, and human coordination that AI can't do alone. This is the 30% that determines whether your work exists in the world or sits in a folder.

The trap: Building Phase 1 and Phase 2 systems but never automating Phase 3.

The Loop

INGEST → COMPLETE → DISTRIBUTE

The production loop is simple in concept: get everything into the system as drafts (INGEST), push each draft through the completion workflow (COMPLETE), then package and deploy the finished product (DISTRIBUTE). The difficulty is in the discipline of actually running the loop.

Case Study: One Bedside Dictation → A Teaching Series

A single clinical dictation — Yamicia speaking into her phone after seeing a patient — generated: a 6-episode clinical teaching series, 9 "clinical atomic units" (standalone teaching moments), and complete EMR documentation for billing. The clinical work is the content substrate. The production loop is what transforms a single input into multiple outputs.

Case Study: The First Article — End-to-End

"That took a very long time. I'm gonna give you what we have now. We still need to do the QC process. I feel like I did an informal QC process. The end product actually came out pretty good."

The first article through the production loop took an entire session — research, drafting, editing, image generation, clinical accuracy review (catching a dangerous recommendation about PROM management), audio recording, formatting, and QC. Subsequent articles will be faster because the loop is now defined.

"For me to realistically be able to do this, we're gonna have to be able to automate more of this. Unless I'm gonna have to hire someone whose full time job is putting these articles together."

The Danger

The Accuracy Problem

The production loop isn't just about speed — it's about catching errors before they reach the public. AI produces confident-sounding output that can contain dangerous errors, especially in technical or clinical domains.

The PROM Recommendation Error

"In my counseling, I never said that waiting up to 12 to 24 hours is a reasonable option. Because that's not a reasonable option. By 12 hours, you really do need to come into the hospital. Where did you get that phrasing? That's why I want you to use my counseling. Because I have to stand by this."

What happened: AI generated a clinical timeline that included a recommendation Yamicia would never make — and that could endanger patients. She caught it because she's a physician. If she weren't, it would have been published. This is why the production loop includes a human QC gate, and why the human in the loop needs domain expertise.

"These should not be that detailed. If I wasn't the one doing it — I mean, it looks good enough. No one else would've caught that."

Workshop Exercises

Build Your Production Loop

Exercise 1: Audit Your 70%-Done Pile

List everything you've started with AI but never finished. Drafts, research, outlines, project plans. How many items are on this list? Now sort them: which ones are closest to done? Which would have the highest impact if completed? Pick one and push it through to completion this week.

Exercise 2: Map Your Completion Workflow

For one type of work you do regularly (an article, a report, a presentation), map every step from "AI draft" to "published/delivered." Include: who reviews it, what format it needs to be in, where it gets published, and how you know it's done. This is your production loop.

Exercise 3: The QC Checklist

Write a 10-item quality control checklist for your domain. What must be true before something you produce goes public? For clinical content: citations verified, recommendations accurate, language appropriate for audience, images correctly sourced. For business content: numbers correct, stakeholders named accurately, links working, brand voice consistent.
