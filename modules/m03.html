Module 03

Writing for Machines

Task Specification as a Teachable Skill

Track: Foundation — "How I Communicate" Duration: 90 min Format: Lecture + Workshop

The Argument

Core Thesis

The quality of AI output is entirely determined by the quality of the task specification. Writing a good task spec is a teachable skill that most people skip — and then blame the AI for bad results.

This module is not about "prompt engineering." Prompt engineering implies a trick — a magic phrase that unlocks better output. That's a misunderstanding of what's happening. What's happening is communication. You are telling a machine what you need, what you know, what you don't know, and what a good result looks like. The more precisely you communicate those things, the more useful the output.

Writing for machines is not harder than writing for people. It's different. Machines don't infer. They don't read between the lines. They don't share your background. They will do exactly what you ask — including the parts you didn't think to mention.

"There is a specific kind of failure that looks like research. It produces data. It generates citations. It has methodology sections and confidence intervals. And it answers the wrong question so precisely that no one notices the wrong question was asked."

The Spectrum

Three Levels of Talking to Machines

Not every interaction with AI needs a formal task specification. What matters is knowing which level you're operating at — and not confusing them.

Level 1: The Conversation

This is what most people do. You open a chat window and talk. You're exploring. You're thinking. You're asking questions you don't have answers to. This is valuable — it's how Yamicia discovered Netlify ("Help me understand Netlify... what can I actually do?"). Conversations are for discovery. Don't try to get production work done in conversation mode.

Level 2: The Dictation

You already learned this in Module 01. You speak your messy, unstructured thinking into a machine and it structures it for you. The dictation is an input — raw material. It is not a task specification. It is the material from which a task specification might be created.

Level 3: The Task Spec

This is formal. This is structured. This is the document you write when you need reliable, replicable output. A task spec says: here is what I need, here is the context, here are the constraints, here is what the output should look like, and here is how I'll know it's done. This is the skill most people never learn.

"I always want something I can copy and paste. Just assume when I ask you a question, I always want something I can copy and paste."

The Structure

Anatomy of a Task Specification

Every task file must be self-contained. The agent should not need to go find other documents. A complete task spec has six sections:

Section What It Does
1. Header Task name, owner, date, priority. Scannable metadata so anyone (human or machine) can triage instantly.
2. Instructions What to do, in numbered steps. Be specific enough that a new agent with no prior context could execute it. If you find yourself saying "you know what I mean" — you haven't been specific enough.
3. Specified Outputs What files, documents, or artifacts should be produced, with exact names and formats. "Create a report" is vague. "Create REPORT_EDITORIAL_STATUS_APR29.md" is a task spec.
4. Operating Rules Constraints, don'ts, and boundaries. "Do NOT create new folders." "Do NOT deploy without approval." "Do NOT change content in files you didn't create." These are the guardrails.
5. Results Log A section for the agent to record what it did. "Chat dies. Everything must be extracted. Write it like you're briefing someone who wasn't in the room."
6. Completion Gate How does the human know this task is done? A checklist of verifiable conditions. "Output file exists." "No deployment errors." "QC checklist passed." If the gate is ambiguous, the task will never close cleanly.

The Failures

Five Ways Your Task Spec Will Fail

1. The Blank Check

"Make me a social media plan." There is no specified output, no constraints, no context. The AI will produce something — and you'll spend more time evaluating whether it's useful than you would have spent writing the plan yourself.

2. The Wrong Question

The AI precisely answers a question you didn't actually need answered. This is the most dangerous failure because it looks like success. The output has citations, structure, and confidence. But it addressed a symptom, not the real problem. Always ask: "What does completing this actually move forward?"

3. The Assumption Bomb

You assumed the AI knew something it didn't. You said "update the dashboard" but didn't specify which dashboard. You said "format it correctly" but didn't define what correct looks like. Every word you leave out is a gap the AI will fill with its own assumptions — and its assumptions are often wrong.

4. The Missing Context

"Agents don't know what exists unless the task spec tells them." If you ask AI to create something without telling it what already exists, it will produce work that conflicts with reality. The task spec must include the context — or at minimum, tell the agent where to find it.

5. The Non-Problem

"Before writing a task, ask: does completing this move the pipeline forward?" People waste enormous energy having AI produce deliverables that nobody needed. The most important skill in task writing is knowing when not to write a task.

Case Study

What Good Task Communication Looks Like

Here is a real exchange between Yamicia and an AI. Watch how she provides context, sets constraints, redirects when the AI goes off track, and demands specific outputs.

Setting Context + Constraints

"We are going to be picking up on the coda optimization project. Right now I have limited time and I need to leave this database in usable form within an hour... so those are my practical constraints."

What she did: Named the project. Stated the time constraint. Defined the minimum viable outcome. In three sentences, the AI knows exactly what kind of session this is — practical, not exploratory.

Redirecting Bad Output

"What is wrong with you? Just make a table I can copy and paste. Just like your other one. How can I get this to stop? I am becoming so frustrated."

What she did: Direct, unambiguous feedback. The AI was producing a format that wasn't usable. Instead of politely accepting it, she told it exactly what was wrong and what the correct format was. Frustration is feedback. Use it.

Correcting the Framing

"These questions are not helping me... I just want to provide clarity around the following things."

What she did: The AI was asking discovery questions that weren't useful. She stopped the line of inquiry and redirected: "Here is what I know. Help me with this." You control the conversation. The AI works for you.

Locking In Rules

"Let's lock in a rule. You always ask me what format I want things in. I very rarely want things in PDF. The formats I want are either plain text or fancy markdown. Those are the two outputs that I want."

What she did: Established a persistent rule that prevents a recurring problem. This is the difference between fixing a problem once and fixing it permanently. Good task communication includes rules that outlive the current session.

Advanced Skill

Precision Framing: Asking the Right Question

The most valuable skill in writing for machines is the ability to ask the question that, if answered, actually moves your work forward. Most people ask the question that's easiest to articulate, not the one that matters.

Start From What You Know

Don't ask "What should I do?" Ask "I know X, Y, and Z. Given those constraints, what are my options for A?" The more you tell the machine what you already know, the less it wastes your time telling you things you don't need.

Name the Mechanism

Don't ask "Why is this happening?" Ask "Is this happening because of A, B, or C?" When you name possible mechanisms, you force the AI to evaluate — not just generate. Evaluation is where AI is actually useful. Generation without constraint is noise.

Ask What the Question Assumes

Every question contains assumptions. "How do I scale my team?" assumes you should scale. "How do I publish more frequently?" assumes frequency is the bottleneck. Before asking AI for answers, interrogate your own questions. The wrong question answered perfectly is still the wrong answer.

Sequence Your Questions

"Let's take this one step at a time." Don't dump 10 questions at once. Sequence them. Let the answer to question 1 inform question 2. This mirrors how you actually solve problems — and it prevents the AI from producing a wall of surface-level responses instead of depth on the thing that matters.

"We don't start from a schedule because we have no basis to make that decision yet. Our assets are the material. The strategy is how we deploy them."

Workshop Exercises

Practice Writing for Machines

Exercise 1: Rewrite a Vague Request

Take this request: "Make me a social media plan." Rewrite it as a self-contained task spec with all six sections (Header, Instructions, Specified Outputs, Operating Rules, Results Log, Completion Gate). The rewrite should be at least 200 words. If it's shorter, you haven't been specific enough.

Exercise 2: The Frustration Test

Find a conversation where AI gave you a bad result. Go back and read your input. Identify: (1) What you assumed the AI knew. (2) What format you expected but didn't specify. (3) What context was missing. Rewrite your input and resubmit. Compare the results.

Exercise 3: Write a Rule

Identify a recurring frustration in your AI interactions — something you have to correct repeatedly. Write it as a persistent rule in the format: "Always [do X]. Never [do Y]. When in doubt, [default behavior]." Add this rule to your AI's system prompt or constitution.

Exercise 4: Interrogate Your Question

Before your next AI interaction, write down your question. Then ask yourself: "What does this question assume? What would change if those assumptions were wrong? Is this the question that, if answered, actually moves my work forward — or is it just the easiest question to ask?"
