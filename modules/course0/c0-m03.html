Conversation, Not Commands

Most people type short prompts and take the first output. Power users have conversations — messy, iterative, nonlinear conversations that produce output indistinguishable from their own thinking. The difference is everything.

How Most People Use AI vs. How This Works

The Command Mode (Most People)

"Write me an email about X."

"Summarize this document."

"Give me 5 ideas for Y."

Transactional. Input a sentence, get an output, copy-paste it somewhere. The interaction is one or two exchanges. The output is generic. Most people don't push back, don't argue, don't refine. They take the first output or give up.

The Conversation Mode (Parallel Genius)

"Here's what I'm thinking about X. What am I missing?"

"That's wrong. The actual clinical protocol is—"

"Now take that framework and apply it to this case."

Iterative. 30-50+ exchanges per session. Correction, refinement, pushback, redirection. The AI holds the thread while the human shapes the output through conversation. The result sounds like the human — because the human's thinking is the raw material.

"I don't use prompts. I never write a sentence and expect an output. There's nothing helpful I can get from that sort of output. My work is too complicated. I've always used these through conversation — not because anyone taught me how to do that, but through trial and error."

Why Conversation Produces Better Output

1. Context Accumulates

Every exchange in a conversation adds context. By exchange 30, the AI has absorbed your preferences, your terminology, your reasoning style, your domain knowledge. A single prompt has none of this. The output quality scales with conversation depth.

2. Correction Is the Work

"That's wrong" is the most productive thing you can say to an AI. Every correction teaches the model what you actually mean, not what it assumed. "Please refer to your knowledge database. James works in an investment firm. There's no such scandal." Correction is not failure — it's calibration.

3. The AI Holds the Thread

When you're interrupted — by kids, by patients, by life — the AI holds the thread of the conversation. "Because I cannot even remember what I just said because my son disrupted me." The AI remembered. The conversation continued. This is why conversation mode is more resilient than prompt mode.

4. Your Voice Stays In

In conversation mode, you're constantly injecting your language, your phrasing, your logic into the exchange. The output absorbs your voice through repetition. In prompt mode, you get the AI's generic voice. "I don't think people understand how much of your language actually has to be put into these projects in order to make them worthwhile."

The Dictation Advantage

The single biggest advantage YC has in using AI is not intelligence, domain knowledge, or technical skill. It's that she dictates.

"I think a lot of people feel really uncomfortable talking to the computer. And the reason I can do it better is because I'm used to dictating. I just dictate clinic notes. You dictate things that you saw. You just get used to talking to yourself. And then you see that work reflected back through the computer."

Before AI: Structured Dictation

"For the last 10 years, I've written like this. I dictate myself, take a transcript — messy just like this — and edit it down. That would be my first draft of papers. I would more or less be in a quiet space and write closer to what I wanted the final thing to look like."

After AI: Nonlinear Dictation

"With AI, I can make it much more nonlinear. Instead of just having a structured conversation, I can go all over the place — brainstorm here, argue there, sketch an idea, come back to it — and the AI organizes it. The editing rounds become shorter."

Surgeons are uniquely positioned for this because they already dictate operative reports — a structured narrative of what happened, dictated in real-time. But anyone who practices dictation can develop this skill. The tool matters too: a dedicated dictation device (like a Plaud Note) is more reliable than an iPhone, which introduces distracting notifications and inconsistent transcription.

The Intelligence Requirement

This is uncomfortable to say, but it's true and it matters for how you set expectations:

"To use AI well, you have to be pretty smart. I've definitely tried to train people on my team to do it, and a lot of people can't. People were waiting for the AI to give them direction. But you need to give the AI direction. So it's just two directionless people."

This is not gatekeeping. It's diagnosis. If the person driving the AI doesn't have a clear thesis, domain knowledge, or the ability to evaluate output quality, the AI will produce confident-sounding garbage and neither party will notice.

The skill isn't "knowing how to prompt." The skill is knowing what you want to say and being willing to say it imperfectly — then working with the AI to refine it into something precise.

Fear is the other barrier. "Fear of what? I'm not sure. The only thing that's a problem is you're gonna waste your own time. And trust me, I've wasted a lot of time. But it allows you to test something at really low cost."

The Proof: One Session, Live

The AI Essays conversation — the source material for this entire Course 0 — was conducted while:

  • Cooking dinner
  • Managing three children (ages 2-5)
  • Negotiating TV show selections ("That's a 3 out of 3. That's a perfect score.")
  • Teaching a 2-year-old to get water from the fridge
  • Dealing with hot milk incidents

The output from that single session: a strategic framework for AI curriculum, a revenue model, a three-article publishing plan, and the entire thesis for what became this curriculum.

That is the Parallel Genius method in action. Not a quiet office. Not a perfect prompt. A conversation, held together by an AI that doesn't care that you're also making dinner.

Workshop Exercises

Exercise 1: The 30-Exchange Conversation

Choose a topic you know deeply. Have a 30+ exchange conversation with an AI about it — not asking it to produce something, but thinking through a problem with it. Push back at least 5 times. Correct it at least 3 times. At the end, ask it to summarize what you discussed. Compare the summary to what you'd have gotten from a single prompt.

Exercise 2: The Dictation Draft

Use voice dictation (phone, dedicated device, or computer microphone) to talk through an idea for 5 minutes. Don't structure it. Don't edit yourself. Include the interruptions, the tangents, the "wait, where was I" moments. Feed the raw transcript to an AI and ask it to organize it into a structured document. Compare that document to what you'd have written from scratch.

Exercise 3: The Correction Log

In your next AI work session, keep a tally of every time you correct the AI. "That's wrong." "No, it's actually..." "Please use the data from..." Count them. At the end of the session, review: which corrections produced the biggest quality improvements? That's your calibration rate — and it's the most undervalued skill in AI productivity.
