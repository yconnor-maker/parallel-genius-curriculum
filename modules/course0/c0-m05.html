What to Build vs. What to Buy

AI tools change every 90 days. Before you invest time building a workflow, ask: will this exist as a feature next quarter? If yes, don't build it. If no, build it today — because that's your competitive advantage.

The Strategic Question

"If the AI companies are gonna build it, I don't want to spend time finding a solution to a problem that is going to be solved in 6 months. We need to work on the problems that have no solution down the pipeline. If something can be bought more easily than I can pay someone to do it — we buy it."

This is the most important strategic question for any small organization using AI. You have limited time, limited budget, and a landscape that changes faster than you can build. Every hour you spend building a workaround is an hour you're betting won't be wasted when the platform ships the feature natively.

The framework is simple: build what they can't, buy what they will.

The Build / Buy / Wait Framework

🛑 Don't Build: Platform Infrastructure

These are the things AI companies are actively racing to ship. Building your own version is waste.

  • Persistent memory across sessions — Anthropic, OpenAI, Google all actively working on this. Months away, not years.
  • Tool orchestration / browser agents — computer use agents already exist. Cowork, Manus (acquired for $2B). Getting smoother fast.
  • Log generation and retrieval — auto-summaries, session continuity — obvious gap every company sees.
  • End-to-end content production — from draft to formatted final asset. Platform-native soon.

✅ Build Now: Your Competitive Advantage

These require your domain knowledge, your judgment, your organizational context. No AI company will build them for you.

  • Domain-specific verification workflows — clinical accuracy checking, regulatory compliance, industry-specific QA
  • Your voice and brand architecture — system prompts, style guides, editorial standards that encode your specific identity
  • Organizational knowledge architecture — the structure of how your team's knowledge connects
  • Cross-agent coordination protocols — how your specific agents hand off to each other
  • Decision authority and escalation paths — when does the AI decide, when does the human decide, when does it escalate

Real Examples From This Organization

Built: The CONDUCTOR.md

A master operating document that gives every AI agent the context it needs to do its job. No AI company will ship your CONDUCTOR. It encodes your team structure, your naming conventions, your deployment targets, your editorial standards. This is yours to build. (Template in the Starter Kit)

Should Not Have Built: CODA/Gamma API Integration

"Our tech people are trying to build integrations through CODA's API, and Gamma's API, and it's taken them a long time. It hasn't been smooth. It's actually quite cumbersome." Meanwhile, browser-based agents that literally click the buttons for you — like Cowork + Chrome — shipped natively. The API integration was a build that should have been a wait.

Built: The Compliance Auditor Pattern

An agent that scans operational infrastructure and measures health metrics. This isn't a generic tool — it's calibrated to this organization's specific schema, naming conventions, and quality thresholds. No platform will ship your auditor.

Correctly Waited: Content Scheduling

Rather than building a custom scheduling system, the team uses existing tools (RightStack) and will integrate through browser agents when that capability matures. The scheduling problem is being solved by the market.

The Role Nobody Hired: Tool Connection Engineer

"Before adopting any of these tools, you need an engineer whose sole job is tool connection. Because that becomes the big issue. You can do one part, then another part in a separate machine, but if someone's not actively always connecting the tools—"

This is a role that emerged from practice, not from a job description. The most common failure pattern in AI adoption is not "the tool doesn't work." It's "the tools don't talk to each other."

You adopt Claude for writing, Gamma for design, RightStack for scheduling, Airtable for data, Slack for communication. Each tool works. None of them are connected. And the person connecting them is usually the CEO — by hand — because nobody else understands the full picture.

The Tool Connection Engineer is the person (or the process) that ensures outputs from one system become inputs to the next. In a small organization, this might be a workflow rather than a person. But the function must exist or everything stays siloed.

The Single Front Door Principle

"I want one front door. I don't want to have to interact with 20 different AI tools. That is tedious in itself. I want essentially a person. A person would do it. I want the computer to click the buttons."

The ideal state is not 20 specialized tools. It's one orchestrator that talks to 20 tools behind the scenes. You give it a brief, it produces the final output. The architecture:

  • Front door: One AI you talk to (your orchestrator)
  • Middle layer: The orchestrator delegates to specialized tools
  • Back end: The tools execute and return results

This is where the industry is heading. Computer use agents — AI that literally opens browsers, clicks buttons, fills forms — are the bridge. You don't need API integrations if you have an AI that can use the same interface you use.

For now, the practical version: minimize the number of tools you interact with directly. Consolidate where you can. Wait for orchestration where you can't.

Workshop Exercises

Exercise 1: The Tool Inventory

List every AI and AI-adjacent tool your organization uses. For each one, note: (a) what it does, (b) what it connects to, (c) who connects it, (d) what data gets lost between it and the next tool. The gaps between tools are where your productivity leaks.

Exercise 2: The Build/Wait Decision

For each custom workflow or integration your team has built (or is planning to build), apply the framework: Is this domain-specific knowledge that no platform will ship? Or is this a general capability that AI companies are actively building? Be honest. Kill the builds that should be waits.

Exercise 3: The 90-Day Forecast

Write down three things you wish your AI tools could do. Research whether any major AI company has announced progress on them. For each one, estimate: will this exist as a feature within 90 days? If yes, don't build around it. If no, it's safe to invest in a custom solution.
