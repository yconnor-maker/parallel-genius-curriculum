Module 05

Visual-First Communication

The Format Hierarchy for Busy Humans

Track: Foundation — "How I Deliver" Duration: 90 min Format: Lecture + Workshop

The Argument

Core Thesis

The format you deliver work in determines whether it gets consumed or ignored. Most people default to long-form text — reports, documents, emails — because that's what they know how to produce. But the person consuming your output doesn't have time to read.

This is especially true when AI is accelerating your output speed. You can now produce 10x more content in the same time. But the human reviewing it still has the same 24 hours. If you deliver everything as text, you've created a bottleneck that no amount of AI can solve.

The solution is a format hierarchy — a priority order that determines how you package information based on the context, the audience, and the urgency.

"Anything YC is supposed to read must be HTML or a proper document format. Markdown is for agents and systems."

The Hierarchy

The Five Formats — In Priority Order

1. Slide Deck — "Flip in 90 Seconds"

The highest-impact format. A well-designed deck communicates complex ideas in under two minutes. The reader controls the pace. Each slide makes one point. No paragraphs. This is the format for stakeholders, decision-makers, and anyone who needs to understand fast.

When to use: Status updates, project briefs, strategic proposals, team briefings.

2. Annotated Images — "Show, Don't Tell"

A screenshot with arrows. A diagram with labels. A table with highlighted rows. Visual annotations communicate what paragraphs of text cannot. The brain processes images 60,000x faster than text. Use this.

When to use: Debugging, design feedback, data analysis, workflow documentation.

3. Clickable HTML — "Self-Guided Experience"

Interactive documents deployed to a URL. Dashboards, microsites, styled reports. The reader can explore at their own pace. This is more work to produce but creates a professional, persistent artifact that lives beyond the conversation.

When to use: Published resources, client-facing deliverables, team tools, curriculum.

4. Audio Summary — "The Review Unblock"

"YC cannot sit at a computer to read articles. Convert EVERYTHING that needs review to audio. This directly unblocks the review bottleneck." Audio lets busy people review work while driving, exercising, or doing clinical rounds. It's the format that converts dead time into productive time.

When to use: Article review, session briefings, meeting summaries, daily dispatches.

5. Long-Form Text — "Last Resort"

The format most people default to. It should be your last choice, not your first. Long-form text is appropriate for published essays, formal documentation, and reference material. It is not appropriate for status updates, feedback requests, or anything time-sensitive.

When to use: Published essays, formal reports, reference documentation, legal/compliance documents.

The Rule

Markdown Is for Agents. Humans Get Formatted Output.

There's a practical version of this hierarchy that matters for AI workflows: the output format you request from AI determines its utility.

If you ask AI to produce markdown, you'll get something that's easy for agents to process but painful for humans to read. If you ask for HTML, you get something deployable and professional. If you ask for a slide deck, you get something people will actually look at.

The Dashboard as Dispatch Center

"The dashboard itself should contain the dispatch prompts needed to spin off new agents, eliminating the need to go anywhere else." The best dashboards aren't just status displays — they're launch pads. One-click access to the actions you take most frequently.

12 Decisions as a Card Deck

YC turned a complex decision framework into a set of cards — each card presents one decision, the options, and the trade-offs. Not a report. Not a document. A card deck you can flip through and mark your choices. The format is the interface.

"Let's lock in a rule. You always ask me what format I want things in. I very rarely want things in PDF. The formats I want are either plain text or fancy markdown."

The Theory

Making Information Stick

Yamicia has a theory about information stickiness that drives her publishing strategy and, by extension, this module:

"There's so much content out there. How do people really decide what is important? How can I create information that's sticky? That's one of the reasons I like fiction writing — it creates a stickiness that makes information penetrable. It's like in studying, right? Why you use case studies and not just an outline in a book."

The principle: Narrative format makes information penetrable in a way that data-first format does not. A case study teaches more than a framework. A story teaches more than a lecture. Format is not decoration — it's the delivery mechanism for comprehension.

Workshop Exercises

Reformat Your Work

Exercise 1: The Format Audit

Look at the last 5 things you sent to a colleague or client. For each one, identify: (1) What format you used. (2) Whether they read it. (3) What format would have been better. Most people will find they defaulted to text for everything — and got ignored for at least half.

Exercise 2: Convert Text to Visual

Take a report, email, or document you've written. Convert it into two alternative formats: a 5-slide deck and an annotated screenshot/diagram. Compare the time-to-comprehension across all three. Which one communicates the core message fastest?

Exercise 3: The Audio Test

Take something that's stuck in your review queue — a draft, a proposal, something you've been meaning to read. Use a text-to-speech tool (ElevenLabs, NotebookLM, or your phone's accessibility features) to convert it to audio. Listen to it while doing something else. Did you finish it? That's the power of format.
