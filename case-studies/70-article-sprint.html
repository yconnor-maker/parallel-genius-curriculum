Case Study

The 70-Article Sprint

How one physician completed 70 articles in two afternoons — and why her team couldn't absorb them. A case study in AI super-productivity and the bottleneck nobody warned her about.

Referenced in: M07, M09 Source: Claude Cowork session Outcome: Production loop redesign

What Happened

The Setup

Yamicia had been building an editorial pipeline for the Labora Collective — a health publishing platform with hundreds of articles in various stages of completion. The backlog was enormous. Articles sitting in drafts, research half-done, outlines never finished.

The Sprint

Using Claude's Cowork feature (which allows the AI to work autonomously on file-based tasks), she processed articles through the pipeline — researching, drafting, editing, and formatting them in rapid succession. In two afternoons, 70 articles reached completion.

The Problem

"My team couldn't absorb it. They're still working through the backlog." The articles were done, but they needed human review — clinical accuracy checks, voice consistency, formatting verification, image creation, and publication decisions. The team could process maybe 5 per day. The backlog would take weeks to clear.

The Realization

The bottleneck had moved. Before AI, the constraint was production speed. Now the constraint was human absorption capacity. Producing faster didn't solve the problem — it moved it downstream.

The Lessons

Lesson 1: Production Speed ≠ Delivery Speed

AI can draft at 10x speed. But the draft isn't the deliverable. The deliverable is the published, reviewed, verified output. If your review capacity is 5/day, producing 70 in two days just creates a 14-day backlog.

Lesson 2: The Team Must Scale With the System

This sprint revealed that the team's workflows were designed for pre-AI production speeds. The solution wasn't to slow down production — it was to build batch-processing systems for review, template the QC steps, and automate the formatting pipeline.

Lesson 3: Batch Production Requires Batch Review

If you're going to produce in batches, you need to review in batches. That means standardized QC checklists, templated review flows, and — critically — the human reviewer needs to be freed from everything except the judgment calls.

"For me to realistically be able to do this, we're gonna have to automate more of this. Unless I'm gonna have to hire someone whose full time job is putting these articles together."

What Changed After

This case study directly led to three curriculum modules:

  • ✦ Module 07: The Production Loop — the INGEST → COMPLETE → DISTRIBUTE framework that prevents 70%-done pileups
  • ✦ Module 09: Protecting the Human — the Super Productivity Paradox and absorption capacity
  • ✦ Module 05: Visual-First Communication — format as a solution to the review bottleneck
