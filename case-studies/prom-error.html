⚠️ Critical Case Study

The PROM Error

When AI generated a clinical recommendation that could have endangered patients — and only the physician caught it. Why human verification is not optional.

Referenced in: M07, M09 Domain: Clinical publishing Severity: Patient safety

The Context

Yamicia was building the Labora Collective's first full-pipeline article — a clinical education piece about PROM (Prelabor Rupture of Membranes), a condition where a pregnant person's water breaks before labor begins. The article was being produced through the editorial workflow: AI-assisted research, drafting, image generation, audio recording, and formatting.

The production system was working beautifully. Research was thorough. The draft was well-structured. Images were generated. The audio script was prepared. Everything looked professional and complete.

Then Yamicia read the clinical timeline.

What the AI Wrote vs. What the Doctor Knew

❌ What AI Generated

"Waiting 12–24 hours can be safe if you and baby are doing well."

This implies that waiting at home for up to 24 hours after your water breaks is a reasonable clinical option.

✅ What the Physician Actually Counsels

"I would like you to come into the hospital. That's my recommendation. If you want to stay at home for a couple hours, that's not unreasonable. However, the longer you're at home — you increase your risk of infection, and that could affect both you and baby."

"24 hours is way long. As a physician, I would start nervously pacing around 12 hours. Asking the nurses — where is she? Can we get her on the phone?"

"In my counseling, I never said that waiting up to 12 to 24 hours is a reasonable option. Because that's not a reasonable option. Where did you get that phrasing? That's why I want you to use my counseling. Because I have to stand by this."

Why This Is Terrifying

The Content Looked Perfect

The article was well-researched. The citations were real. The language was professional. The clinical framework was sound. A non-physician reviewer would have approved it. A non-physician editor would have published it. The error wasn't obvious — it was buried in the nuance of clinical judgment.

The Error Was Dangerous

A pregnant person reading this article might decide to wait 24 hours at home after their water breaks, believing it's clinically safe. It isn't. Prolonged rupture increases the risk of infection — chorioamnionitis, sepsis, NICU admission. This recommendation could lead to real harm.

The AI Was Confident

The AI didn't flag uncertainty. It didn't say "this recommendation may vary by clinical context." It presented the 12-24 hour window as established guidance. This is the confidence problem from Module 10: "The technology will not announce that it is operating from an impoverished conceptual base."

"If I wasn't the one doing it — I mean, it looks good enough. No one else would've caught that."

The Lessons

1. Domain expertise is the QC layer

AI can draft. AI can research. AI cannot make clinical judgments. The human in the loop needs to be the domain expert — not a general reviewer, but someone who knows the difference between "12-24 hours can be safe" and the actual clinical standard of care.

2. Use your voice, not AI's

After catching this error, YC mandated: "Use my counseling. My actual words." AI should structure and format — but the clinical content must come from verified human input, not AI-generated approximations of what a doctor might say.

3. Verification must be structural

This wasn't caught by a QC checklist. It was caught because the right person happened to read the draft carefully. A production system can't rely on "happened to." Verification must be a required gate in the pipeline — not an optional step.

4. Scientific images have the same problem

"These should not be that detailed. If I wasn't the one doing it, no one else would've caught that." AI-generated medical diagrams contained inaccuracies in the same article. The visual content needed the same expert review as the text.

What Changed After

This case study established three permanent rules in YC's production system:

  • ✦ Clinical content must use verified human input. AI can format and structure — but the medical recommendations come from YC's actual counseling language, not AI paraphrase.
  • ✦ Domain expert review is a pipeline gate. No clinical article publishes without physician review. This is structural, not optional.
  • ✦ Scientific images require the same scrutiny. AI-generated medical visuals are treated as drafts that require expert validation, not finished assets.
