"I Don't Use Prompts."

A visual essay on the philosophy that built this curriculum.

Dr. Yamicia Connor · Parallel Genius
scroll ↓
"I never write a sentence and expect an output. My work is too complicated. I've always used these through conversation."— Dr. Yamicia Connor, dictated while managing three children

Most people interact with AI the way they interact with Google.

Type a question. Get an answer. Close the tab.

That's a search engine. And it works — for search engine problems. Simple questions with definitive answers.

But the work that matters — the work that builds businesses, publishes research, creates curriculum, manages teams — that work doesn't fit in a single question.

The prompt economy tells you:

"Write the perfect prompt and AI will give you the perfect answer."

This is a lie.

Not because prompts don't work. They do — for simple things. But because the premise is wrong. The quality of AI output is not proportional to the quality of your prompt. It's proportional to the quality of your thinking.

The Prompt

"Write me a business plan for a women's health education company."

One input. One output. Generic. Template-driven. Sounds like every other business plan AI has ever generated.

Time: 30 seconds.
Value: Low.

The Conversation

"I'm a physician who's been building this for two years. Here's what I've tried. Here's what failed. Here's the market gap I see that nobody else is addressing..."

30 exchanges later, the AI has your constraints, your failures, your unique insight. The output sounds like you.

Time: 30 minutes.
Value: Transformational.

Not prompts. Conversations. Some lasted 5 minutes. Some lasted 3 hours. Every one of them was a dialogue — a back-and-forth where thinking happened out loud.

"The idea of speaking your unfinished thoughts into a system and having them organized, challenged, and refined feels alien. It feels vulnerable. It feels like you're exposing incomplete thinking."

That's exactly the point.

AI is most useful when you give it your incomplete thinking. Not your polished prompt. Your messy, contradictory, half-formed ideas. Because the AI doesn't judge the mess — it organizes it.

Here is the thing nobody tells you about the dictation method:

It was developed by a surgeon.

Surgeons dictate operative reports — speaking structured, precise language into a recording device while their hands are occupied. They've been doing this for decades.

The skill is the same. Think out loud. Speak with precision. Let the system handle the formatting.

The difference is that now the system talks back.

Each message is a correction, a clarification, a pushback, a refinement. Each one calibrates the AI's understanding of who you are and what you need.

"It's not like you don't work. It's not like you go on vacation and the AI is there generating articles. That's not how it is."— Dr. Yamicia Connor

The marketing says AI replaces work. The reality is that AI changes the shape of work. You stop typing and start thinking. You stop formatting and start deciding. You stop executing and start directing.

The work doesn't disappear. It transforms. And if you're not ready for the transformation, you drown in production you can't absorb.
